1900 – 1914: The New Century & early Modernism

By 1900, Art Nouveau had lost much of its dynamism through sheer imitation and
repetition. Additionally, several events have caused the decline of this artistical
movement. The death of Toulouse-Lautrec in 1901 and the abandonment of poster
art by Mucha and Cheret (who both turned to painting) left a void that was filled
by a young Italian caricaturist named Leonetto Cappiello who arrived in Paris in
1898. Strongly influenced by Cheret and Toulouse-Lautrec, Cappiello rejected
the fussy detail of Art Nouveau. Instead he focused on creating one simple
image, often humorous or bizarre, which would immediately capture the viewer's
attention and imagination on a busy boulevard. He appreciated the overriding
need to create instant visual impact, as exemplified by his 1906 poster designs
for Maurin Quina absinthe. This ability to create a brand identity established
Cappiello as the father of modern advertising, and details of functionality and
visuals can be witnessed in the poster design of 21st century in all spectrums.
The style of Cappiello would dominate Parisian poster art until A.M. Cassandre's
first Art Deco poster in 1923.

Meanwhile, from 1905, artists working in Scotland's Glasgow School, Austria's
Vienna Secession, and Germany's Deutscher Werkbund also were transforming
Art Nouveau's organic approach, and focused towards a simplier, more
functional style. These schools rejected curvilinear ornamentation in favor of a
rectilinear and geometric structure based on functionalism. More and more
poster artists switched from the elegant Art Nouveau to geometric styles, to
sharpen the advertising message. A key outgrowth of these modernist efforts
was the German Plakatstil, or Poster Style, which was begun in 1905 by Lucian
Bernhard in Berlin and in Munich by Ludwig Hohlwein. Minimalized naturalism
and emphasis on flat colours and shapes made their work the next step towards
creating an abstract, more modern visual language, so that the communication
with the audience was more advanced than ever before.

Ludwig Hohlwein was good at eliminating all non-essential graphics, because
he was noted for his use of shadow versus light, as well as his portrayals of
people and animals. On the other hand, Lucian Bernhard is another figure
in the history of poster design that should not be left out. Bernhard's contribution
to the development of modern design, advertising and culture in the early
20th century can be summed up in his poster design for the Priester match
company. However, that piece was just the launching pad for his entire career.
His advertising work amassed a client list that included Cat's Paw, Westinghouse,
ExLax, Bosch, Manoli, Priester, Adler, Amoco and Grand Rapids Furniture
Company. In a time when a great deal of theory was developing behind the fields
of graphic design and advertising Bernhard worked almost entirely by instinct,
saying “You see with your eyes, not your brain.”
