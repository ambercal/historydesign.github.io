1890-1900 : The Belle Epoque & Art Nouveau

During the 1890s, referred to as the Belle Epoque in France, poster exhibitions,
magazines and dealers proliferated. In 1891, Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec's
extraordinary first poster, Moulin Rouge, elevated the status of the poster to fine art
and touched off a poster craze. Recording the performances he viewed and the
establishments he visited on a nightly basis, he functioned as artist and narrator:
his paintings, drawings, prints, and posters expose the complexities of the quickly
changing age in which he lived. Lautrec found great success in the medium of
poster art, as he managed to produce over 350 lithographic posters and illustrations.
Despite descending from three lines of aristocracy, Lautrec derived artistic inspiration
from the people he lived among in Montmartre’s working-class neighbourhood,
including prostitutes, singers, and fellow artists.

Nevertheless, poster artists were transforming Parisian streets into colourful art galleries
for the public, which featured a magnificent matter of Parisian night life, attaining cult
status in the process, and causing theatre stars to insist on choosing their own favourite
artist to do the poster for their show. Interest in the poster was further enhanced by the
emergence of Art Nouveau during the 1890s, a decorative style of art marked by
flowing, curvilinear shapes, and drawing inspiration from Byzantine icons, Pre-Raphaelite
romanticism and the Celtic Art Revival movement. Largely reliant upon form, line and colour,
Art Nouveau proved the ideal poster design, and dominated the Parisian poster scene up
until the late 1900s. Not long after, Alphonse Mucha, a Czech artist working in Paris,
created the first masterpieces of Art Nouveau in both fine arts and poster design.
Bearing multiple influences including the Pre-Raphaelites, the Arts and Crafts Movement,
and Byzantine art - this flowering, ornate style became the major international
decorative art movement up until World War I. In comparison to today, everyone recognizes
Mucha ‘s work – the inspirational level, that most produce, is magnificent.

Moreover, in each country, the poster was used to celebrate the society's unique cultural
institutions. Like mentioned before, in France, the cafe and cabaret was omnipresent.
As to such countries like in Italy - the opera and fashion; in Spain the bullfight and
festivals; in Germany trade fairs and magazines, in Britain and America literary journals,
bicycles, and the circus. Despite cross-pollination, distinctive national styles also
became apparent - Dutch posters were marked by restraint and orderliness, and Italian
poster was recognized by their drama and grand scale for their directness and medieval
influence. Again, it is important to mention, that despite these differences from country to
country, all of them shared similarities because of then ongoing trends in art world, as
well as the way the audience is perceived.
